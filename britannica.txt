                   <p class="topic-paragraph">
                    <strong>
                     <span id="ref180507">
                     </span>
                     Pittsburgh
                    </strong>
                    ,
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/city">
                     city
                    </a>
                    , seat (1788) of
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Allegheny">
                     Allegheny county
                    </a>
                    , southwestern
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Pennsylvania-state">
                     Pennsylvania
                    </a>
                    , U.S. The city is located at the
                    <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off mw" data-term="confluence" data-type="MW" href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/confluence">
                     confluence
                    </a>
                    of the
                    <span id="ref180508">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Allegheny-River">
                     Allegheny
                    </a>
                    and
                    <span id="ref180509">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Monongahela-River">
                     Monongahela
                    </a>
                    rivers, which unite at the point of the “
                    <span id="ref180510">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Golden-Triangle-region-Pittsburgh-Pennsylvania">
                     Golden Triangle
                    </a>
                    ” (the business district) to form the
                    <span id="ref180511">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Ohio-River">
                     Ohio River
                    </a>
                    . A city of hills, parks, and valleys, it is the centre of an urban industrial complex that includes the surrounding cities of
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Aliquippa">
                     Aliquippa
                    </a>
                    (northwest),
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/New-Kensington">

                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Washington-Pennsylvania">
                     Washington
                    </a>

                  <section data-level="1" id="ref241999">
                   <h2 class="h1">
                    History
                   </h2>
                   <!--[PREMOD2]-->
                   <span class="marker PREMOD2 mod-inline">
                   </span>
                   <p class="topic-paragraph">
                    Algonquian- and Iroquoian-speaking peoples were early inhabitants of the region. The conflict between the British and French over territorial claims in the area was settled in 1758 when General
                    <span id="ref742096">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Forbes">
                     John Forbes
                    </a>
                    and his British and colonial army expelled the French from Fort Duquesne (built 1754). Forbes named the site for the British statesman
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Pitt-the-Elder">
                     William Pitt the Elder
                    </a>
                    . The British built
                    <span id="ref1298235">
                    </span>
                    Fort Pitt (1761) to ensure their dominance at the source of the
                    <a class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Ohio-state">
                     Ohio
                    </a>
                    . Settlers began arriving after
                    <a class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Native-American">
                     Native American
                    </a>
                    forces led by Ottawa chief
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Pontiac-Ottawa-chief">
                     Pontiac
                    </a>
                    were defeated in 1763; an agreement subsequently was made between Native American groups and the Penn family, and a boundary
                    <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off eb" data-term="dispute" data-type="EB" href="https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/dispute">
                     dispute
                    </a>
                    was ended between Pennsylvania and Virginia. Pittsburgh was laid out (1764) by John Campbell in the area around the fort (now the Golden Triangle). Following the
                    <a class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/event/American-Revolution">
                     American Revolution
                    </a>
                    , the town became an outfitting point for settlers traveling westward down the Ohio River.
                   </p>

                   <p class="topic-paragraph">
                    Pittsburgh’s strategic location and wealth of natural resources spurred its commercial and
                    <span id="ref180512">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/event/Industrial-Revolution">
                     industrial growth
                    </a>
                    in the 19th century. A
                    <a class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/blast-furnace">

                    </a>

                    <span id="ref843134">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/steel">
                     steel
                    </a>
                    industry that for more than a century was the city’s economic mainstay; by 1850 Pittsburgh was known as the “Iron City.” The Pennsylvania Canal and the Portage Railroad, both completed in 1834, opened
                    <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off eb" data-term="vital" data-type="EB" href="https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/vital">
                     vital
                    </a>
                    markets for trade and shipping. The city suffered a great loss in 1845 when some 24 blocks of businesses, homes, churches, and other buildings were destroyed by fire.

                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/AFL-CIO">

                    <a class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II">
                     World War II
                    </a>
                    , the war years bringing a particularly great boon for the economy. The population

                     crested
                    </a>

Most citizens were still of European ancestry, but the growing African American proportion of the population exceeded one-fourth. During the period of economic and
                    <a class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/science/population-growth">

                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/science/nuclear-energy">

                  <section data-level="1" id="ref242000">
                   <h2 class="h1">
                    The contemporary city
                   </h2>
                   <!--[PREMOD6]-->
                   <span class="marker PREMOD6 mod-inline">
                   </span>
                   <p class="topic-paragraph">
                    By the late 1970s and early ’80s, the steel industry had virtually disappeared—a result of foreign competition and decreased demand. Many of the surrounding mill towns were laid to waste by
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/money/unemployment">
                     unemployment
                    </a>
                    , becoming a symbol of the
                    <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off mw" data-term="notorious" data-type="MW" href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/notorious">
                     notorious
                    </a>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Rust-Belt">
                     Rust Belt
                    </a>
                    , the U.S. region where steelmaking and
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/manufacturing">
                     manufacturing
                    </a>
                    once thrived before
                    <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off mw" data-term="succumbing" data-type="MW" href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/succumbing">
                     succumbing
                    </a>
                    to widespread unemployment and
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/poverty">
                     poverty
                    </a>
                    . Pittsburgh, however, successfully diversified its economy through more emphasis on light industries—though metalworking, chemicals, and plastics remained important—and on such high-technology industries as computer software, industrial automation (robotics), and biomedical and environmental technologies. Numerous industrial research laboratories were established in the area, and the
                    <a class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/money/service-industry">
                     service sector
                    </a>
                    became increasingly important. Pittsburgh long has been one of the nation’s largest inland ports, and it remains a leading transportation centre.
                   </p>

                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Pittsburgh">
                     University of Pittsburgh
                    </a>

                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Carnegie-Mellon-University">
                     Carnegie Mellon
                    </a>
                    (1900),

                    </a>

                   <p class="topic-paragraph">
                    Central to the city’s cultural life is the
                    <span id="ref664938">
                    </span>
                    Carnegie Museums of Pittsburgh (formerly Carnegie Institute), an umbrella organization consisting of a number of institutions. Its museums include those for the fine arts and natural history (both founded in 1895), the Carnegie Science Center (1991), which now also houses the Henry Buhl, Jr., Planetarium and Observatory (1939), and the
                    <span id="ref843136">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Andy-Warhol">
                     Andy Warhol
                    </a>
                    <span id="ref1298233">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/The-Andy-Warhol-Museum">
                     Museum
                    </a>
                    (1994), which exhibits the works of the Pittsburgh-born artist and filmmaker. Other institutions
                    <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off mw" data-term="affiliated" data-type="MW" href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/affiliated">
                     affiliated
                    </a>
                    with the organization are the
                    <span id="ref843141">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Carnegie-Library-of-Pittsburgh">
                     Carnegie Library of Pittsburgh
                    </a>
                    , which contains more than 3.3 million volumes, and the Carnegie Music Hall. The
                    <span id="ref1298234">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pittsburgh-Symphony-Orchestra">
                     Pittsburgh Symphony Orchestra
                    </a>
                    performs at Heinz Hall, a restored movie theatre.
                   </p>

                    <span id="ref1305106">

                    Phipps Conservatory and Botanical Gardens (1893), which covers 15 acres (6 hectares), is noted for its extensive greenhouses. The
                    <span id="ref1305107">
                    </span>
                    city’s zoo, in the northeastern Highland Park neighbourhood, includes an aquarium. Two new sports
                    <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off mw" data-term="venues" data-type="MW" href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/venues">
                     venues
                    </a>
                    opened in 2001 on the north bank of the Allegheny opposite the Golden Triangle:
                    <span id="ref1305108">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/PNC-Park">
                     PNC Park
                    </a>
                    is home of the
                    <span id="ref843138">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pittsburgh-Pirates">
                     Pirates
                    </a>
                    , the city’s professional
                    <a class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/sports/baseball">
                     baseball
                    </a>
                    team, and
                    <span id="ref1305109">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Acrisure-Stadium">
                     Acrisure Stadium
                    </a>
                    houses the
                    <span id="ref843139">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pittsburgh-Steelers">
                     Steelers
                    </a>
                    , its professional
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/sports/American-football">
                     football
                    </a>
                    team. The
                    <span id="ref843140">
                    </span>
                    <a class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pittsburgh-Penguins">
                     Penguins
                    </a>
                    , Pittsburgh’s professional
                    <a class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true" href="https://www.britannica.com/sports/ice-hockey">
                     ice hockey
                    </a>
                    team, plays at
                    <span id="ref1305110">
                    </span>
                    PPG Paints Arena. Popular summertime attractions include riverboat excursions on Pittsburgh’s waterways and Kennywood, an amusement park southeast of the city in West Mifflin.
                   </p>
